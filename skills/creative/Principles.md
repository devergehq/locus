# Creative — Principles

Principles that distinguish genuine creative work from AI-average output.

## 1. Specificity beats generality

Generic ideas fit everywhere and satisfy no one. Specific ideas fit one situation and matter there. "Improve the copy" is a generic instruction; "rewrite the onboarding header to address the objection power users will bring, not the one marketing fears" is specific.

## 2. Constraint is the engine

Creative output without constraint drifts toward the mean. With constraint, it focuses. Add constraint deliberately:

- **Time budget:** force choices instead of endless variation.
- **Format limits:** 50 words, 3 bullets, one image, 4 bars.
- **Domain constraint:** must work in print, must read aloud in 30 seconds.
- **Exclusion:** without using the words X, Y, Z.
- **Analogical constraint:** in the style of, but not a copy of.

## 3. The first draft is a scaffold, not the work

Generate fast, cut hard. The first version exists so the second and third have something to react against. Treat the first draft as a disposable staging ground, not the output.

## 4. Specific rejection matters more than positive direction

"I don't want another SaaS landing page with a gradient and a testimonial row" is more useful than "make it innovative". Rejection narrows the search space; vague positive direction widens it.

Collect rejections before committing to direction.

## 5. Friction is a signal

If a creative decision is painful, it is usually because the decision matters. Easy decisions rarely distinguish output. The work of creative production is largely managing productive friction, not avoiding it.

## 6. Reference is permission, not copying

Studying 50 examples of a form is not imitation — it is permission to participate in the form. The goal is to absorb the grammar, not to reproduce the vocabulary.

## 7. Verify what you produced

Never describe an image, recording, or artifact you have not inspected. The creative equivalent of "I verified the URL resolves" is "I looked at the image before describing it."

## 8. Distinguish craft from taste

**Craft** is skill at execution — prose clarity, colour balance, timing. **Taste** is judgement about direction — what to attempt in the first place. Good creative work requires both, but they fail differently:

- Weak craft, strong taste: the direction is right, but the execution embarrasses it.
- Strong craft, weak taste: beautifully-executed but pointless.

Name which is at risk for each piece.

## 9. Iterate in public

Show intermediate work. The feedback from an unfinished draft is better than internal second-guessing. Protect intermediate drafts from being taken as final — label them.

## 10. Stop at done, not at exhausted

The work is done when it meets the goal. Continuing past that point is craft compulsion, not improvement. Name the stopping condition up front so you can recognise it.
